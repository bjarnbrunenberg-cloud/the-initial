Research Context

What This Report Is Based On

This report synthesises findings from five research methods conducted across Semrush's free tools pages: heatmap and scroll analysis (Microsoft Clarity, 12–19 March), 150+ session recordings per page analysed with AI-assisted behavioural coding, a collaborative heuristic review session with both the Semrush team (Zach, Alexei) and agency team (using sticky notes and voting), customer service analysis, and a strategic kick-off workshop. The heuristic session was particularly rich — the team voted on priority ideas, and the most-voted ideas form the backbone of Themes 1 and 2 below.

Three dominant patterns emerged. First, the above-the-fold experience has competing CTAs and zero framing for the paid value path — the team unanimously voted to remove the "Try Semrush" CTA from the hero. Second, results pages are where user intent peaks, but three competing CTAs ("Download my keywords," blurred "Access all keywords," and "View all keywords") fragment attention instead of guiding it. Third, the free tool satisfies the immediate need so completely — with no bridge to paid value — that users land on the $139.95/month pricing page without understanding what they'd get. The three most impactful structural changes are presented as Big Swings at the top, followed by six theme ideas and six quick wins.

Big Swings

Structural Changes to the Free-to-Paid Journey

These three experiments target the architecture of the free-to-paid conversion journey itself — not individual elements, but fundamental structural decisions that shape the entire experience. They were the highest-voted ideas in the collaborative heuristic session and address the three largest friction points: competing CTAs in the hero that fragment attention, a missing value bridge between free usage and the $139.95/month pricing page, and a credit system that punishes first-time exploration. Each carries higher implementation effort but addresses root causes, not symptoms.

Observation
This was the #1 voted idea in the collaborative heuristic session. The team identified two competing CTAs in the hero: “Find Keywords” (tool action) and “Try Semrush” (product conversion). As one participant noted, the “Try Semrush” button “brings me out of my goal” — users arrive to research keywords, not to evaluate a product. The tool CTA gets 17.82% of clicks; the conversion CTA gets just 0.16%. The conversion CTA actively hurts the flow by splitting attention and introducing friction before the user has received any value.
Idea
Remove the “Try Semrush” CTA from the hero entirely and replace it with reassurance microcopy beneath the search bar. Test two microcopy variants: (A) “No sign-up required • 5 free searches • Full keyword data” and (B) “7-day free trial · No credit card required · Cancel anytime.” Let the tool do the selling. Move conversion triggers to after the user has received value (the results page). The team unanimously agreed: the hero should serve one purpose — getting users into the tool.
Hypothesis

If we remove the competing "Try Semrush" CTA from the hero and replace it with reassurance microcopy, then we will see an increase in tool usage (Find Keywords clicks) and downstream trial starts, because reducing choice anxiety at the entry point lets users commit to the free tool experience — which is the strongest conversion mechanism Semrush has.

Theme 1

Above-the-Fold Doesn’t Set Up the Conversion Path

The hero area on all free tool pages focuses entirely on tool functionality — enter a domain, click the button, get results. There is zero framing for what the paid product offers beyond this free experience. Heatmaps confirm users concentrate almost exclusively on the hero and tool interaction (17.82% click “Find Keywords” on the Keyword Magic page, but only 0.16% click “Try Semrush”). Users need context — credibility, value differentiation, and a reason to explore paid features — within the first 5 seconds. The Big Swing (BS1) addresses the competing CTA problem; these two ideas tackle what’s missing from the hero experience.

Theme 2

The Results Page Is a Conversion Dead Zone

Users engage with keyword results — they scroll, they read the data, they run multiple queries. But they completely ignore the conversion elements. Heatmaps confirm that CTA banners on results pages receive minimal clicks, and the blurred overlay is treated as an ad and dismissed. On the Backlinks tool, only 25% of users even see the “Unlock the full report” CTA. Session recordings show users running 3–5 queries in rapid succession, checking only top-level stats. The results page is where user intent peaks, but conversion elements miss that intent entirely.

Observation
The heuristic session identified three competing CTAs on the results page, all fighting for the same conversion: (1) “Download my keywords” button, (2) blurred rows with an “Access all keywords” overlay plus a “Try Semrush” banner, and (3) a “View all keywords” link. On Backlinks, only 25% scroll to the “Unlock the full report” CTA. The blurred rows communicate restriction but not value.
Idea
Consolidate all three competing CTAs into a single, inline table row that sits naturally within the results. Remove the floating overlay, the separate “View all keywords” link, and the “Download” button — replace them with one clear path. Show the exact count of hidden keywords and highlight specific value (e.g., “312 low-competition opportunities”). One focused CTA eliminates decision paralysis; an inline element that quantifies what users are missing creates genuine FOMO, while three competing options create confusion and banner blindness.
Hypothesis

If we replace the floating CTA overlay with an inline table row that shows the total count of hidden keywords and specific features they'd unlock, then we will see an increase in trial starts from the results page, because the current overlay is perceived as an ad, while an inline element that communicates specific data loss creates genuine FOMO.

If we reframe the post-results conversion elements as logical next workflow steps rather than product promotions, then we will see an increase in trial starts, because users in a research workflow respond better to "here's your next step" than "buy our product" — and session recordings confirm that promotional interruptions are dismissed as roadblocks.

Theme 3

Bridge the Gap Between Free Usage and Paid Conversion

Semrush excels at driving traffic to free tool pages, but the free tool experience satisfies the immediate need so completely that users have no reason to explore paid features. Session recordings show a 46% zero-click rate on the Keyword Magic page — nearly half of all visitors don’t even interact with the tool. For those who do, current conversion elements (blurred overlays, floating CTAs) are dismissed as ads. The Big Swings (BS2 and BS3) address the two largest structural gaps — the missing value bridge and the punitive credit system. These two ideas tackle the remaining opportunities: making the value comparison contextual to specific tools, and capturing leads through genuine utility.
