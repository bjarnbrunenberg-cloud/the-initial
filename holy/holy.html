Research Context

What This Report Is Based On

This document presents 11 experiment ideas for de.holy.com, derived from a strategic analysis that included a hands-on website audit, KPI tree decomposition, competitive benchmarking against 5 direct DTC competitors (AG1, Huel, MyProtein, NOCCO, Foodspring), and a test bandwidth analysis across HOLY's 4 highest-traffic pages. The analysis identified Conversion Rate and Subscription Rate as the two highest-leverage metrics in HOLY's revenue KPI tree.

Two big strategic swings emerged — launching a subscription model (HOLY's single biggest untapped revenue stream) and redesigning the Mix & Match experience (HOLY's unique proposition that currently underperforms). Beyond these, three themes of tactical experiment ideas target the product page value story, first-time visitor understanding, and small friction points that are silently leaking revenue across the funnel.

Strategic Bets That Change the Revenue Model

These aren't incremental optimisations — they're structural changes to HOLY's business. A subscription model unlocks predictable recurring revenue in a category where every competitor already leads with subscriptions. A Mix & Match redesign turns HOLY's unique proposition from a confusing page into a conversion engine that drives higher AOV. These two initiatives have the potential to add €4.3M+ in annual recurring revenue and measurably increase average order value.

Observation
HOLY has no visible subscription model on de.holy.com. Every major competitor (AG1, Huel, MyProtein, NOCCO, Foodspring) leads with subscriptions. Subscription customers have 2–3× higher LTV and 70–80% lower CAC. For a consumable product that runs out every 2–4 weeks, this is the single biggest structural gap.
Idea
Launch a phased subscription model: Month 1 validates demand with a painted door test (zero backend investment). Month 2 ships an MVP with “cancel anytime” messaging. Month 3+ builds the retention flywheel with post-purchase upsells and reorder nudges.
Hypothesis

If we validate and launch a phased subscription model starting with a painted door test, then we will unlock a new recurring revenue stream worth up to €4.3M ARR, because HOLY's consumable product with 2–4 week replenishment cycles is the textbook subscription use case — and every competitor has already proven the model works in this category.

Theme 1

The Product Page Undersells HOLY's Strongest Value Props

HOLY's PDP receives 110,000 weekly visitors at an 11.7% conversion rate — making it the single highest-revenue page on the site, with nearly €2M in annual testing impact. But the current layout buries HOLY's most compelling selling points: a price-per-serving advantage that is 4× cheaper than Red Bull, massive social proof from 2 million customers and an FC Köln partnership, and the natural scarcity of limited-edition flavours. Every percentage point of CR improvement on this page translates directly to revenue.

Observation
HOLY costs ~€0.35 per serving — 4× cheaper than Red Bull and 5× cheaper than AG1. But nowhere on the PDP does this comparison appear. The price “€24.99 for a bag of powder” creates sticker shock without context.
Idea
Add a visual price-per-serving comparison strip directly on the PDP, showing HOLY alongside Red Bull, AG1, and a café latte. Make the cost advantage impossible to miss.
Hypothesis

If we add a price-per-serving comparison strip below the product title on PDP, then we will see an increase in add-to-cart rate, because the current absolute price (€24.99) creates sticker shock that disappears when reframed as €0.35/serving against €1.50+ alternatives — and competitor benchmarking confirms that value-framing is the strongest purchase driver for first-time buyers of powder-format drinks.

Theme 2

New Visitors Don't Understand What HOLY Is

HOLY's homepage sees 132,000 weekly visitors at just 9.1% conversion rate — the lowest of all key pages. The hero focuses on slogans and campaigns, but a first-time visitor still doesn't know: what is this product? How does it work? What's the difference between Energy, Hydration, Iced Tea and Milkshake? This conceptual gap is killing conversion before visitors even reach a product page. The newcomers page exists but converts at only 11.2%, suggesting the onboarding path needs fundamental rethinking.

Theme 3

Small Friction Points Are Silently Leaking Revenue

Not every conversion leak is a big strategic gap. Some are small UX friction points that compound across thousands of sessions: a quiz that overwhelms rather than guides, reviews buried where nobody sees them, product cards that frustrate because visitors can't figure out where to click. These are quick wins — fast to implement, easy to measure, and they often deliver surprising lift because the fix is so obvious in hindsight.

Observation
The collection page has 40K weekly visitors at 15.9% CR — the highest CR of any page, suggesting high intent. But with 20+ energy flavours on a single page and no filtering beyond manual scrolling, visitors with decision fatigue may leave. There is no guided recommendation mechanism.
Idea
Add an interactive 3-question flavour quiz at the top of the collection page that narrows 20+ options down to 3–5 personalised recommendations, reducing decision paralysis at the moment of highest purchase intent.
Hypothesis

If we add an interactive flavour quiz that filters the collection to 3–5 personalised recommendations, then we will see an increase in collection-to-cart conversion, because reducing 20+ options to a curated shortlist reduces decision fatigue and increases purchase confidence — a principle well-documented in choice architecture research (Iyengar & Lepper, 2000).

Only the product image and title on product cards are clickable. The description, price, and card background are dead zones. Without a clear CTA button, visitors may find it difficult to know where to click to reach the PDP. This creates unnecessary friction on every single product card.
Idea
Make every element on the product card clickable — image, title, description, price, and card background. The entire card becomes a link to the PDP. An alternative is to add a visible CTA button to each card.
Hypothesis

If we make every element on product cards clickable (image, title, description, price, card background), then we will see an increase in PDP visits from collection pages, because reducing click friction enables visitors to navigate to the product they're interested in faster — and every dead click is a micro-frustration that compounds across thousands of sessions.
