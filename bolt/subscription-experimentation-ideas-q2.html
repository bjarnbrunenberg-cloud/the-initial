Subscription Pricing & Architecture

These two experiments target the pricing architecture of the subscription model itself. CS data confirms price is the #1 barrier (57% chose Bolt for price), and user interviews reveal deep anxiety about long-term costs ("cost of subsequent doses is a major concern"). Bolt's own retention data shows patients who survive Month 1 stay 4–6 months — yet the current pricing model offers no incentive to commit past that threshold. Numan already proves market appetite with 12-month commitment tiers. The pricing structure is the experiment.

Observation
With 89.6% mobile traffic, the current plans page is a vertical scroll marathon. Session recordings show users spending 10+ minutes scrolling 3-4 times between Wegovy and Mounjaro — comparing prices, benefits, and side effects simultaneously. Heatmaps confirm only 52% of mobile users even see the Mounjaro CTA. The page forces product selection AND subscription decision AND checkout intent into a single overwhelming scroll. CS data confirms the #1 barrier is price (57%), and retention data shows the Month 1 churn cliff — but the current single-price model offers no commitment incentive.
Idea
Replace the monolithic post-quiz page with a 3-step mobile wizard: Step 1 — choose your product (Wegovy vs Mounjaro stacked, thumb-friendly cards). Step 2 — choose your subscription tier (3-Month "Most Popular" shown first, then Monthly Rolling, then 6-Month, with weekly pricing). Step 3 — checkout. Hypothesis

If we replace the monolithic post-quiz page with a 3-step mobile wizard (product → subscription tier → checkout), then we will see higher subscription conversion, shorter decision time, and improved 90-day retention, because 89.6% of users are on mobile where session recordings show 10+ minutes of scrolling between products (decision overload), only 52% see both options, and the current page bundles three decisions into one infinite scroll. Separating product choice from subscription commitment into focused mobile screens reduces cognitive load — while the commitment tiers (weekly pricing: £27.50 "Most Popular", £29.75, £24.75) with anchoring on the 3-month plan incentivise users to commit past the critical Month 1 churn cliff.

THEME 1: SUBSCRIPTION FRAMING

The Subscription Isn't Framed as a Trial

The drop rate is highest after the first month of service — but patients who continue past Month 1 typically stay 4–6 months until they reach their goal. The primary quit reason is pricing, with patients chasing competitor first-customer discounts. Bolt already offers £40 off the first month, but it's framed as a "promo discount" rather than a risk-free trial. Reframing the subscription entry as a low-commitment trial could bridge the Month 1 churn gap and shift the psychological framing from "subscription commitment" to "low-risk trial."

Observation

THEME 2: ENTRY-PAGE SUBSCRIPTION MESSAGING

Entry Pages Don't Sell the Subscription Value

The /baxl-sb page currently shows pricing but doesn't actively sell the subscription value. There's no outcome framing, no daily cost anchoring, no social proof about subscriber retention. Research shows users entering via this page spend 10+ minutes on the plans page comparing options — indicating the entry page isn't priming them for the subscription decision. First impression testing (N=150) showed users' #1 missing information: "how it works" (30%), "pricing" (20%). The entry page has a unique opportunity to frame the subscription as simple, affordable, and backed by evidence.
