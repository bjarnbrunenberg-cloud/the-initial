Step 1

Welcome + lightweight consent

Replaces: Old intro page (screen 1) + old Disclaimer contract page (screen 2). Two screens become one.

What's different

  • Cream background gives Bolt identity from the first second — not a generic white form.
  • The disclaimer content is kept verbatim in meaning but reframed as first-person commitments instead of legal bullets.
  • Pre-ticked checkboxes signal "this is light" — the user confirms by tapping the CTA.
  • The word "DISCLAIMER" is gone. So is the contract vibe.

Why it works

User research flagged "disclaimer fatigue: repetitive copy and disclaimer-like pages slowed users and decreased motivation." This pattern preserves the five commitments at the start of the flow, while removing the two heaviest psychological barriers on the first touchpoint.

Gabriel: "We can remove this page as long as we're using the information here somewhere else." Done — five statements present, same meaning, lighter vehicle.

Step 2

Age + biological sex

Replaces: Old screen 3 (age yes/no) + old screen 6 (biological sex). Two related questions, one screen.

What's different

  • Age range updated to 18–84 — the 74–84 band is eligible with clinician review, which today's 18–74 gate excludes.
  • Inline info icon covers the "why we ask" for biological sex without a subheading block.
  • Both questions are mandatory and take seconds — a single screen saves a tap with no loss.

Why it works

Age and biological sex share a "demographics" mental frame. Grouping them removes a full tap+transition while making no answer harder. The tooltip handles the clinical rationale for sex without a full explainer paragraph.

Gabriel: "Age question is a must… we updated to 84 as well. Between 74 and 84 it's a matter of clinical review. Biological sex, also important — we have some logic if they're a woman."

Step 3

Height, weight, ethnicity

Replaces: Old screen 5 (BMI) + old screen 7 (ethnicity). Two screens become one.

What's different

  • Height, weight, and ethnicity share a screen — they are the three inputs that together define BMI eligibility.
  • Ethnicity sits below the measurements with a short "why we ask" line, not as a separate subheading block.
  • No mid-quiz BMI readout. The result is calculated silently and surfaced at submission — hard-stop signals stay at submission level where they belong.

Why it works

Grouping the three inputs that jointly determine eligibility keeps the mental model coherent. The silent-calculation pattern respects the rule that eligibility signals must not be visible mid-quiz: a user who sees they're borderline today can't reverse-engineer their answers tomorrow.

Gabriel: "We don't want them to know why we hard-stop them. Otherwise, they can just go back in the quiz and change the answers."

Step 4

Safety-critical conditions

Replaces: Old screen 8 (one of three near-identical "Do you have any of the following conditions?" pages).

What's different

  • Prominent "None of these apply" at the top — the majority with no conditions tap once and move on. Today they have to scan a flat list of 18 items first.
  • Grouped by category — allergies, heart & circulation, digestion, other. Users scan by theme, not line-by-line.
  • Every condition is still a separate checkbox. The visual grouping changes perception, not the data model.
  • Copy reframed from clinical language to "we ask to keep you safe" — same meaning, friendlier tone.

Why it works

Users with no conditions (the majority case) finish the screen in one tap. Users who do have conditions scan a categorised list rather than 18 flat items. Hard-stop rejection happens at submission — nothing here tells the user their tick blocked them.

Gabriel: "These three questions are very important, but we can frame them in a different way if needed… we have to be granular here." Visual grouping only — data stays granular.

Step 5

Other conditions + weight-loss reasons

Replaces: Old screen 4 (motivations) + screen 9 (past/present conditions) + screen 10 (lower-BMI conditions) + screen 11 (any other health conditions open). Four screens become one.

What's different

  • Colour-coded groups — blue for clinician-review, green for lower-BMI eligibility. Users know the stakes of each tick before they make it.
  • The current quiz asks three near-identical "Do you have any of the following conditions?" pages in a row. Colour + section labels fix the "where am I?" confusion.
  • Motivations question kept intact with all 6 options plus Other. It feeds lower-BMI eligibility logic — any non-Other answer can qualify a user at a lower threshold when combined with ethnicity and conditions, so all six need to stay capturable.
  • The open "anything else" text field is an inline toggle — users who want to add context can, users who don't never see a text area.

Why it works

Three serial screens of condition-asking become one screen with three visually distinct blocks. This is where users currently feel the quiz's length most. The motivations question stays because its clinical function (BMI eligibility nudge) cannot be compressed without losing data on the 5 out of 6 answers that aren't "managing a specific health condition."

Gabriel: "If they are white and they have some conditions OR reasons for wanting to lose weight, they might be eligible for this BMI criteria. If they say Other, they are not going to be eligible." The full question preserves that logic exactly.

Step 6

Medications (current)

Replaces: Old screen 12 (structured medication list) + old screen 13 (other medications yes/no + open). Two screens become one.

What's different

  • Same structured list users see today — every hard-stop drug (insulin, sulfonylureas) remains as its own checkbox.

Step 7

Weight-loss history (progressive disclosure)

Replaces: Old screen 14 (prior GLP-1) + screens 15, 16, 17 (four conditional follow-ups). Up to five screens become one.

What's different

  • Users who have never tried GLP-1 treatment select "None of these" and the Next button advances. No follow-up screens imposed.
  • Users with history see the four follow-ups expand inline on the same screen. Still one step. All four clinical inputs captured.
  • Weight-at-start, last dose timing, current dose, and side effects — every data point needed for dose continuation is preserved.

Why it works

Today's flow gives everyone the same multi-screen path regardless of whether they need it. This step branches invisibly: one answer for users with no history, an expanded panel for users who need to continue treatment. All four follow-ups land on the same screen, so there's no "how many more to go" effect.

Try the radios — selecting a specific medication opens the follow-up panel. Selecting "None of these" closes it. This is the largest screen collapse in the whole redesign.

Step 8

Anything else + acknowledgement

Replaces: Old screen 18 (anything else to share yes/no + open) + old screen 19 (full Acknowledgement Statement contract page). Two screens become one.

What's different

  • The current acknowledgement page is a wall of legal bullets users tap "Next" on without reading. This redesign makes the consent explicit: a tick above the CTA is required.
  • The four commitments are shown in scannable format with green ticks. Full legal text available in a modal via "View full terms" link.
  • The "anything else?" optional text field shares the screen — users who skip it proceed in one tap.

Why it works

Today the acknowledgement "consent" is just a Next tap, indistinguishable from every other Next tap in the quiz. The explicit tick pattern turns it into a real agreement moment while taking less visual space than a full page of bullets. Full terms stay one tap away via the modal link for users who want to read them.

Gabriel: "It could be a page on the website — we can just have 'by clicking next you accept the acknowledgement statement.'" This design takes that same direction, with an explicit tick instead of an implicit next tap.

Step 9

Register

Replaces: Old registration page. Minor polish, no structural change.

What's different

  • Headline reframed from administrative ("Please Register as a New Patient") to value-delivering ("Where should we send your consultation results?"). Repositions the form as the pay-off, not a chore.
  • CTA copy: "Review my consultation" — again, progress framing.
  • Trust strip below the CTA surfaces the reassurances users said they were looking for in interviews ("they looked for Trustpilot + licensed treatments").
  • Marketing consent checkbox is unchecked by default.

What didn't change

  • Same four fields.
  • Same validation.
  • Same submission path (to hard-stop handling or success page).

Open questions for Gabriel

Nine specific clarifications to lock in before engineering. None block Phase 1 (intro + disclaimer + progress + acknowledgement), but each affects Phase 2 decisions.

1

The exact BMI-eligibility rule around motivations and conditions

"White + (conditions OR reasons for losing weight) → BMI 26.5" was the rough summary. Which specific answers to the motivations question count as a qualifying "reason"? Any non-Other answer, or only specific ones? Confirms whether the motivations question can ever be compressed.

2

Is any BMI feedback acceptable mid-quiz?

This design calculates BMI silently and surfaces nothing at the question level. If a neutral readout ("we'll review after submission") is acceptable, we can add a reassurance moment back. Confirm the rule.

3

Can age + biological sex share one screen?

Both questions are mandatory. Combining them saves one step. Any clinical reason to keep them separate?

4

Can height/weight + ethnicity share one screen?

Ethnicity is sensitive. Giving it its own screen signals the "why we ask" framing explicitly. Any reason not to combine?

5

Age duplication with pre-landing

Age appears to be asked on pre-landing AND in the quiz. Confirm: is this real? Which one is the source of truth? Can one be removed?

6

Condition groupings on Step 4

Proposed groups: Allergies · Heart & circulation · Digestion · Other. Confirm these match how Bolt's clinicians think about these conditions.

7

"Last dose" answer ranges on Step 7

Proposed buckets: This week / 1–2 weeks / 1+ month / 3+ months. Confirm these are the meaningful thresholds for dose-continuation logic, or switch to a date picker.

8

Explicit-tick consent pattern on Step 8

The compliance owner should confirm an explicit tick above the CTA is acceptable. Gabriel's suggestion was "by clicking next you accept" — this goes one step stronger.

9

Per-question drop-off data from Embeddables

Pull the actual drop-off per current question. Phase 2 sequencing should follow the real data — the biggest friction screens get consolidated first.
