Research Context

What This Report Is Based On

This document consolidates findings from multiple research streams: user research sessions with 5 participants who navigated the weight loss treatment journey end-to-end, a heuristic analysis workshop involving Bjarn, Reinhard, Gabriel, Nathan, and Christina, PostHog heat maps and session recordings, CX team feedback on recurring patient questions, and data collected through weekly catchup sessions.

Three core themes emerged directly from user research — treatment uncertainty, mismatched user expectations, and post-funnel noise. Two additional themes surfaced during the heuristic workshop: first-page friction (the pre-landing page is losing 40% of traffic before users even enter the funnel) and the lack of guide rails through the commitment journey. Four "big swing" experiments target structural changes to the funnel that could shift conversion materially if validated.

Structural Changes to the Funnel

These four experiments target the architecture of the weight loss journey itself — not individual elements, but fundamental structural decisions that shape the entire experience. They carry higher implementation effort but address the largest drop-off points in the funnel. Each one emerged from converging signals across multiple research sources.

Theme 1

Reduce Treatment Uncertainty

Users consistently wanted verifiable proof that Bolt Pharmacy is a legitimate, regulated pharmacy. GPhC registration numbers were buried at the bottom of the page where nobody scrolls. Clinical claims like "22.5% weight loss" had zero source references. Trust signals were scattered, inconsistent, and invisible at the moments that matter most — when users are deciding whether to commit their health (and money) to an online pharmacy.

Theme 2

Match User Anticipation

Users arrive expecting guidance on which treatment is right for them, but the site presents two nearly identical product cards with no decision support. Dosage options are listed without context on what each dose means or when to change. The funnel assumes users already know what they want — but user research showed they need help choosing. The 50% drop-off on the plans page is a direct result of this expectation mismatch.

Theme 3

Reduce Noise After the Decision Point

Once users decide they are interested, the funnel introduces new friction: a competitor comparison table that triggers scepticism, opaque pricing that creates hidden-fee anxiety, and a consultation start page that reads like a legal document. These elements are meant to reassure — but they actually introduce new objections. The content between "I'm interested" and "I'll start" needs to build confidence, not create doubt.

Theme 4

First-Page Friction Is Killing 40% of Traffic

The pre-landing page (/prelanding30) is where the largest volume of users enters the funnel — and where 40% of them leave. The page uses a split-screen layout that forces unnatural left-right reading on desktop. Copy is not aligned with the full funnel proposition. An age gate adds friction before users have seen any value. This page was identified during the heuristic workshop as the single biggest untapped opportunity for improving overall funnel throughput.

Theme 5

The Commitment Journey Has No Guide Rails

As users move through the funnel, small signals send the wrong message. Red pricing creates negative associations for what should be a positive savings moment. The quiz starts with no preparation — users go from browsing to a 19-question medical questionnaire with no transition. These "micro-signals" compound throughout the journey, each one adding a small amount of friction that, together, erode the confidence users need to commit to a monthly health plan.
